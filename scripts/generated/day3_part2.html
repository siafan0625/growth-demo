层级1：初生

Level 1: Nascent

At this stage, a founder's primary job is to find a problem worth solving for three to five customers. And by problem we mean an issue that is urgent and important for them, where solving it would be immensely valuable for their business.

在这个阶段，创始人的主要工作是为三到五个客户找到一个值得解决的问题。我们所说的问题是指对他们来说紧急且重要的问题，解决这个问题对他们的业务将有巨大的价值。

And while it's not the primary focus of Level 1, you should also be thinking about demand here, exploring whether you'll be able to find enough customers with that problem who will need your product.

虽然这不是Level 1的主要焦点，但你也应该在这里考虑需求，探索你是否能够找到足够多的有那个问题需要你的产品的客户。

In our experience, we've found that a fairly high percentage of startups get stuck at Level 1 — and stay stuck for six, 12, or even 18 months. That's when we've seen founders get bogged down in 'the grind.'

根据我们的经验，我们发现相当高比例的初创公司卡在Level 1——并且卡住六个月、12个月甚至18个月。那时我们看到创始人陷入苦战。

That's because the hallmark of this level is that it lacks repeatability — that's to be expected at Level 1. Finding the marginal customer is the opposite of easy.

这是因为这个层级的标志是缺乏可重复性——这在Level 1是意料之中的。找到边际客户绝非易事。

感觉如何

What it Feels Like

As we talked to sales-led B2B founders about how they navigated this first phase, unsurprisingly, several different paths emerged. Some grappled with finding the right product, others struggled to identify the target user, still others doubted the size of the market or even abandoned their original idea entirely.

当我们与销售主导的B2B创始人谈论他们如何度过第一阶段时， unsurprisingly，出现了几条不同的道路。有些人在寻找合适的产品上挣扎，另一些人在识别目标用户上遇到困难，还有一些人怀疑市场规模甚至完全放弃了他们原来的想法。

The common thread was wasted cycles before they stumbled upon those first few sparks of traction — which were more in the vein of 'aha moments' or 'this might just work' inklings, rather than hard metrics and the beginning of a hockey stick growth curve.

共同点是，在他们偶然发现最初的几个牵引力火花之前浪费了周期——这些更像是顿悟时刻或这可能行得通的预感，而不是硬指标和曲棍球棒增长曲线的开始。

Ironclad：通过实地拜访客户解锁更具体的产品

Ironclad: Unlocking a more specific product by visiting customers IRL

Ironclad is now a well-known digital contracting platform (last valued at $3.2B), but unpacking their journey to nascent product-market fit highlights just how much manual, non-scalable work you'll find at this level.

Ironclad现在是一个知名的数字合同平台（最近估值32亿美元），但解析他们通往初生产品市场契合度的旅程突显了在这个层级你会发现多少手动的、不可扩展的工作。

After serving as outside counsel for startups, co-founder Jason Boehmig started tinkering with automating parts of his own practice with coding that he had taught himself along the way.

在担任初创公司的外部法律顾问后，联合创始人Jason Boehmig开始尝试用他自学的编码来自动化他自己业务的部分工作。

现在我们正处于AI助手热潮中，回想起来这很疯狂，因为Ironclad的第一个版本是一个法律文件的自动化助手——但实际上是我在电子邮件地址的后端操作。

It's wild to think back to this now that we're in the AI assistant boom because the first version of Ironclad was an automated assistant for legal paperwork — but it was really me on the backend of an email address.

But it was spending time in person with their initial customers that helped narrow their product's focus.

但正是与最初客户面对面花时间帮助他们缩小了产品的焦点。

我记得我们飞了整个公司——当时只有四五个人——去博尔德见一个对我们有很多产品反馈的客户。

I remember that we flew out the whole company — which was four or five people at the time — to Boulder to see a customer who had a bunch of product feedback for us.

在Ironclad，早期的顿悟时刻来自观察我们的客户使用产品。这使我们能够选择一个我们可以追求的细分受众——法律运营——以及他们正在做的具体任务——常规交易。

At Ironclad, the early 'aha moments' came from watching our customers use the product. It enabled us to pick a niche audience that we could go after — legal operations — and the specific task they were doing — routine transactions.

Verkada：深入到极致，快速行动以打动客户

Verkada: Going unreasonably deep and moving fast to wow customers

Verkada has six different physical security product lines today, but the founders started back in 2016 with video security. Inspired by IoT devices in the consumer space, they realized the enterprise equivalent of applying software to video security was missing.

Verkada现在有六种不同的物理安全产品线，但创始人在2016年从视频安全开始。受到消费领域物联网设备的启发，他们意识到将软件应用于视频安全的企业级等效产品是缺失的。

When co-founder Filip Kaliszan thinks back to the nascent stage of their journey to PMF, the team's speed and willingness to get in the field stand out.

当联合创始人Filip Kaliszan回想起他们通往PMF旅程的初生阶段时，团队的速度和进入现场的意愿脱颖而出。

第一个版本看起来很滑稽。它是一个基于树莓派的相机。我们只是用不同的零件拼凑在一起——供应链是Amazon.com。我们大概建了100个这样的小相机，然后把它们送给了我们认识的朋友和企业。

The very first version looked very funny. It was a Raspberry Pi based camera. We just hacked it together from different parts — the supply chain was Amazon.com. We built maybe 100 of these little cameras and we gave them to friends and businesses we knew.

对于最初的几位客户，我们只是尽一切努力让客户尽快部署，然后与这位客户保持密切联系，了解什么对他们有效，什么无效。

For the first few customers, we just did whatever it took to get the customer deployed as fast as possible and then stayed close to that customer to learn what was working for them and what wasn't.

当你有任何早期需求拉动的迹象时，你必须跟随它，并尽一切努力让它发生。

When you have any signs of early demand pull, you have to follow it and work as hard as it takes to just make it happen.

Plaid：在未经验证的市场中转型并建立信念

Plaid: Pivoting and building conviction in an unproven market

Plaid is now known as the pioneer of the 'invisible plumbing' fintech infrastructure that powers everything from paying your friends back on Venmo, to making an investment on Robinhood, to buying Bitcoin on Coinbase. But the product actually started out as a consumer budgeting app.

Plaid现在被称为隐形管道金融科技基础设施的先驱，它支撑着从在Venmo上还钱给朋友，到在Robinhood上投资，再到在Coinbase上购买比特币的一切。但这个产品实际上最初是一个消费者预算应用程序。

消费者的反应让我们很快意识到我们不擅长构建预算应用程序。我们尝试了一段时间。我们构建了六个不同版本的预算管理，分析你的支出类型的应用程序，但它们从未真正获得任何吸引力。

The response of consumers was such that we pretty quickly realized we weren't going to be any good at building budgeting apps. We tried for a while. We built six different versions of budgeting management, analyzing your spend types of applications and they didn't really ever get any traction.

有一天，一位Venmo早期工程师的朋友来找我们说，嘿，你们的消费产品不是很好，但我想授权你们构建的后端，你们与银行集成以将数据获取到应用程序中的方式。

One day, a friend who was one of the early engineers at Venmo came to us and said, 'Hey, your consumer products are not so good, but I'd like to license the backend that you've built, the way that you've integrated with the banks in order to get the data into your app.'

当我回想起Plaid产品市场契合度旅程的最早阶段时，挑战不是找到人们会购买的产品。而是围绕这个产品创建一个市场，我们相信这对未来非常重要，但尚未得到验证。

When I think back to the earliest stage of Plaid's product-market fit journey, the challenge wasn't finding a product that people would buy. It was creating a market around the product that we believed would be very important for the future, but was yet to be proven.

看起来如何：初生需求、满意度、效率案例研究

What it Looks Like: A Case Study on Nascent Demand, Satisfaction, Efficiency in Action

To return to the very beginning of our example from Looker's actual trajectory, the data platform startup spent roughly from August 2011 to March 2013 in the nascent stage.

回到我们Looker实际轨迹示例的最开始，这个数据平台初创公司大约在2011年8月至2013年3月期间处于初生阶段。

我们等了将近一年才筹集资金，直到我们知道这是一家风险企业——并非每个初创公司都是，你不想被锁定。我们与客户和收入一起努力前进。

We waited almost a year to raise money, until we knew it was a venture business — not every startup is, and you don't want to get locked in. We were cranking along with customers and revenue.

Here's how the business looked at the nascent stage: With that early glimmer of traction, Looker raised a $2 million seed round from First Round and PivotNorth in August 2012. By the end of Q1 of 2013, the 9-person team was at $357K in ARR with 15 customers.

以下是业务在初生阶段的样子：凭借早期的牵引力微光，Looker于2012年8月从First Round和PivotNorth筹集了200万美元的种子轮。到2013年第一季度末，这个9人团队的ARR达到35.7万美元，拥有15个客户。

But to show an even more detailed snapshot of what 'good' looks like at this level, we present a case study on our first fictitious startup: Ledgerly, a platform that uses LLMs to help accounting teams.

但为了展示在这个层级好是什么样子的更详细的快照，我们介绍了第一个虚构初创公司的案例研究：Ledgerly，一个使用LLM帮助会计团队的平台。

The Ledgerly team is pretty new, with just five full-time employees. They raised a $1.2 million pre-seed round earlier this year and they don't even know what their burn multiple is — but that's appropriate, given how early they are.

Ledgerly团队很新，只有五名全职员工。他们今年早些时候筹集了120万美元的pre-seed轮，他们甚至不知道自己的燃烧倍数是多少——但考虑到他们处于多么早期的阶段，这是合适的。

Let's walk through each of the dimensions of product-market fit:

让我们逐一了解产品市场契合度的各个维度：

• Demand: They only have four customers, all of which they acquired through warm intros from their personal networks or from investors. They don't have an outbound sales machine yet.

• 需求：他们只有四个客户，都是通过他们个人网络或投资者的热情介绍获得的。他们还没有外呼销售机器。

• Satisfaction: All of their customers are starting to use the product regularly, at least once a week. Two of them have onboarded multiple users internally.

• 满意度：他们所有的客户都开始定期使用产品，至少每周一次。其中两个已经在内部引入了多个用户。

• Efficiency: This is the area the team has paid the least attention to, which is appropriate given their stage. Customer onboarding feels too lengthy (currently at eight weeks).

• 效率：这是团队关注最少的领域，考虑到他们的阶段，这是合适的。客户入职感觉太长（目前为八周）。

Benchmarks

While the above hopefully gives you a sense of what nascent product-market fit feels like, we've also collected these benchmarks that indicate your company is at Level 1 so you can more closely compare your progress.

虽然上述内容 hopefully 让你对初生产品市场契合度的感觉有了了解，但我们也收集了这些基准指标，表明你的公司处于Level 1，以便你可以更密切地比较你的进展。

• Your team is less than 10 people.

• 你的团队少于10人。

• You're at the pre-seed or seed-stage.

• 你处于pre-seed或种子阶段。

• Demand source is mostly friends and network, some cold outreach.

• 需求来源主要是朋友和网络，一些冷 outreach。

• Sales conversions: 1 out of every 10-20 warm intros converts to a customer.

• 销售转化率：每10-20个热情介绍中有1个转化为客户。

• 3-5 customers.

• 3-5个客户。

• $0-$500K ARR.

• No renewals yet (too early). No regretted churn (some non-regretted).

• 还没有续约（太早）。没有遗憾流失（一些非遗憾流失）。

最重要的是什么

What Matters Most

To reach Level 2, founders need to focus on satisfaction most. In the case of our fictitious startup, Ledgerly, in order to move the needle on satisfaction, their goal is to increase daily use and number of users at each company.

要达到Level 2，创始人最需要关注满意度。对于我们虚构的初创公司Ledgerly来说，为了提高满意度，他们的目标是增加每个公司的日常使用量和用户数量。

With satisfaction front and center, demand is second at this stage. While you don't need heaps of customers, you do need an inkling that if you make this small set of customers happy, eventually there will be that proverbial line out the door.

以满意度为首要，需求在这个阶段排在第二位。虽然你不需要大量客户，但你确实需要一种预感，即如果你让这一小群客户满意，最终会有传说中的门外排长队。

Efficiency comes in last at this stage. We'd recommend that you don't spend a ton of time thinking about it at Level 1.

效率在这个阶段排在最后。我们建议你在Level 1不要花太多时间思考它。

早期与客户保持密切联系是培养这种满意度的关键。但这里有一个重要的区别。如果你没有给他们价值，他们不想靠近你。

Sticking close to your customers early on is key to cultivating that satisfaction. But there's an important distinction here. If you're not giving them value, they don't want to be near you.

对于Looker来说，寻找产品市场契合度是关于弄清楚如何交付价值。对于我们的前四个客户，我有不同的方式来交付我正在构建的东西，从纯咨询到独立产品。

For Looker, the search for product-market fit was about figuring out how to deliver the value. For our first four customers, I had different ways of delivering what I was building, from pure consulting to a standalone product.

我毫不犹豫地将不成比例的资源投入到这里。在SaaS中，专注于让客户成功是一种保留策略，而不是成本中心。

I had no qualms about sinking a disproportionate amount of resources here. In SaaS, focusing on making your customer successful is a retention strategy, not a cost center.

需要注意的警告信号

Yellow Flags to Watch Out for

It's hard to tell in the moment if you're mistaking a more tepid response for early traction, or if an extra dose of founder grit will unlock the breakthrough your business needs.

很难判断你是否将更冷淡的反应误认为是早期牵引力，或者额外的创始人毅力是否会解锁你的业务所需的突破。

许多早期初创公司面临的最大挑战是他们在客户满意度方面对自己撒谎。在这个阶段，一个常见的陷阱是有喜欢但不爱或不需要你的产品的客户。

The biggest challenge for a lot of early-stage startups is that they lie to themselves about customer satisfaction. It's a common trap at this stage to have customers who like but don't love or need your product.

我在Persona内部经常使用关系类比：知道某人喜欢但不爱某样东西是非常非常困难的，但如果你曾经处于朋友区，你就知道那个答案。

I use the relationship analogy a lot internally at Persona: knowing when someone likes but doesn't love something is very, very difficult but if you've ever been in the friend zone, you know that answer.

Here are the yellow flags we recommend watching out for:

以下是我们建议注意的警告信号：

• Customers respond that if your product disappeared tomorrow, they wouldn't be disappointed.

• 客户回应说，如果你的产品明天消失，他们不会失望。

• Usage is low and not growing over a period of six months.

• 使用量低，并且在六个月的时间内没有增长。

• You have a handful of happy customers, but the most important feature is actually different for each of them.

• 你有少数满意的客户，但对他们来说最重要的功能实际上是不同的。

• The sales cycles take too long, and if your champion quits the company, then the deal just falls apart.

• 销售周期太长，如果你的支持者离开公司，交易就会崩溃。

如果你卡住了，你摆脱初生阶段的唯一方法是大的改变，而不是小的改变。很多人在初生阶段徘徊，多年来只做小的改变，这实际上是在浪费每个人的时间和资源。

If you're stuck, your only way out of the nascent stage is big changes, not small changes. A lot of people hang out in nascent and make small changes for years, and that's just a literal waste of everybody's time and resources.

通常情况下，如果你正在做的事情没有得到很好的牵引力，你可能不是离10%的调整——你可能离200%的调整更远。

More often than not, if you're working on something that is not getting great traction, you're probably not a 10% adjustment away — you're probably a 200% adjustment away.

层级2：发展中

Level 2: Developing

By Level 2, you've ticked through some of the classic milestones, such as building something 20 or so customers need, or getting to your first $1M in ARR. And while you haven't totally figured it all out, there's also much more repeatability in your customers' needs, the messaging that resonates, and the product solution you're delivering.

到了Level 2，你已经完成了一些经典的里程碑，比如构建大约20个客户需要的东西，或者达到你的第一个100万美元ARR。虽然你还没有完全弄清楚一切，但你的客户需求、引起共鸣的信息传递以及你交付的产品解决方案都有了更多的可重复性。

Developing PMF is when you're starting to see glimmering signs that the marginal customer is more within reach — but it by no means feels easy yet. This is one of the reasons why Level 2 is one of the two levels that the majority of companies get stuck on.

发展PMF是指你开始看到边际客户更容易触及的微光迹象——但这绝不意味着感觉容易。这是Level 2是大多数公司卡住的两个层级之一的原因之一。

感觉如何

What it Feels Like

当时市场拉动是什么样子非常明显。有些人在从未接触过真实产品的情况下就向我们支付年度预付合同，只是基于我们的设计模型——这与我们在OKR方面的经历完全不同。

It was very obvious what market pull looked like then. We had people paying us annual upfront contracts without ever touching a real product, just on our design mocks — which couldn't be more different than the experience we'd been having with OKRs.

有潜在客户从各地自然涌来。人们在向他们的朋友推荐我们。人们会说，我两周后要启动这个绩效周期，我需要这个功能。你们还没有。我们明天能安排时间讨论一下，以便我们帮助你们构建它吗？

There were leads coming organically from all over the place. People were telling their friends about us. People would say, 'I'm kicking off this performance cycle in two weeks and I need this feature. You don't have it yet. Can we schedule time tomorrow to talk it through so we can help you build it?'

一旦你有了可行的东西，即使面对一个相当不成熟、不完整、半损坏的产品，它也会奏效，而我们在这个阶段当然有这样的产品。

Once you've got something that's going to work, it's going to work even in the face of a pretty undeveloped, incomplete, semi-broken product, which we certainly had at this stage.

看起来如何：发展中的需求、满意度、效率案例研究

What it Looks Like: A Case Study on Developing Demand, Satisfaction, Efficiency in Action

Let's rewind the clock back to when Looker was at Level 2, roughly from March 2013 to Nov 2014. The startup emerged from stealth in March 2013 with a 34-person team and 20 customers, many of which were also in the First Round community.

让我们把时间倒回到Looker处于Level 2的时候，大约从2013年3月到2014年11月。这家初创公司于2013年3月从隐身模式中脱颖而出，拥有34人的团队和20个客户，其中许多也在First Round社区中。

By August 2013, they'd made enough progress to raise a $16M Series A round. By November 2013, they'd hit the coveted $1M in ARR milestone and grown to over 40 customers.

到2013年8月，他们已经取得了足够的进展，筹集了1600万美元的A轮融资。到2013年11月，他们达到了令人垂涎的100万美元ARR里程碑，并增长到40多个客户。

As for our example company at this level, meet Hire Hero, a fictional interviewing platform that uses AI to help teams hire better. They have got 18 full-time employees. They recently raised a $10M Series A round, and they've got a burn multiple of 4.2x.

对于我们在这个层级的示例公司，让我们认识Hire Hero，一个虚构的面试平台，使用AI帮助团队更好地招聘。他们有18名全职员工。他们最近筹集了1000万美元的A轮融资，他们的燃烧倍数是4.2倍。

Let's walk through each of the dimensions of product-market fit:

让我们逐一了解产品市场契合度的各个维度：

• Demand: They're at $1.2M in ARR and they have 22 customers. Their average sales cycle is 32 days, but that's an improvement over 47 days.

• 需求：他们的ARR为120万美元，有22个客户。他们的平均销售周期是32天，但比47天有所改善。

• Satisfaction: This team has been around long enough to actually track renewals, and four of their customers have renewed. Weekly active usage is going up.

• 满意度：这个团队已经存在足够长的时间来实际跟踪续约，他们的四个客户已经续约。每周活跃使用量正在上升。

• Efficiency: At this stage, it's still not a focus, but at least they know they're at 65% gross margin. Their onboarding has gotten 15% faster.

• 效率：在这个阶段，它仍然不是重点，但至少他们知道他们的毛利率是65%。他们的入职速度提高了15%。

Benchmarks

• Team size: Up to 20 people.

• 团队规模：最多20人。

• You're either a Seed or Series A company.

• 你是种子轮或A轮公司。

• Demand source: Early signs of a scalable channel that doesn't depend on warm intros.

• 需求来源：可扩展渠道的早期迹象，不依赖于热情介绍。

• 5-25 customers.

• 5-25个客户。

最重要的是什么

What Matters Most

To level up, your priority needs to shift to demand. Here's why: We've seen true grind-it-out founders muscle their way to 20 customers through sheer willpower and effort. But you can't will your way to 100 or 200 customers — the satisfaction and demand for the product are starting to do the work for you.

要升级，你的优先级需要转向需求。原因如下：我们看到真正的苦干型创始人通过纯粹的意志力和努力强行获得20个客户。但你无法凭意志力获得100或200个客户——产品的满意度和需求开始为你工作。

口碑很棒，但在B2B中，你还需要扩展获取客户的方式。你需要比你想象的更早开始这项工作。

Word of mouth is wonderful, but in B2B you also need to scale ways you're acquiring customers. You need to start on that work earlier than you think.

需要注意的警告信号

Yellow Flags to Watch Out for

• Current customers are happy, but you're having trouble opening the floodgates.

• 当前客户很满意，但你在打开闸门方面遇到困难。

• Regretted churn is greater than 20%.

• 遗憾流失大于20%。

• Starting to get concerned about your burn multiple.

• 开始担心你的燃烧倍数。

• Sales cycle is taking too long and you lose deals late in the funnel.

• 销售周期太长，你在漏斗后期失去交易。
